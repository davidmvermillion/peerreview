Background ⇧

2

Exoplanets are planetary bodies that exist outside of our solar system. This report examines statistical data from the collection of all known exoplanets. Please note that the GUI-generated SQL query where this data came from is incomplete. However, with that understanding, this report presents some interesting findings. The report starts with histogram distributions, presents some part-of-the-whole data in pie charts (to work within the constraints of the report generator being used), provides some scatterplots, and finishes off with a table of summary statistics.

3

Percentage Distributions ⇧

12

Looking at percentages, 90% of exoplanets are in a system with only one star. Figure 6 shows that most exoplanets orbit around one star. However, there are 2, 3, and 4 star systems with planets orbiting them. Additionally, because of data quality issues with this dataset, while some rogue planets do not orbit a star, those were not included in the exported data for this report. Figure 7 shows that most exoplanets exist by themselves in their planetary system. However, there are systems with 2 or more planets orbiting them.

Scatterplots ⇧

16

Some of the highlighted objects are best viewed as scatterplots. Figure 8 shows that most exoplanets' orbit sizes are within an order of magnitude to Earth's orbit (1 AU). Figure 9 shows that most exoplanets are within 2,000 parsecs of Earth, with some significantly further out. Figure 10 shows that most exoplanets have circular orbits. However, there are a sizeable number of planets with more elliptical orbits. In fact, several have very elliptical orbits (eccentricity approaching 1).

Conclusion ⇧

24

We didn't start discovering exoplanets until 1989. However, since then, we have discovered a lot of exoplanets. This report only examines a handful of the confirmed exoplanets discovered to-date. Most discovered exoplanets are about 450 parsecs from Earth, orbit one star very closely and nearly circularly, exist by themselves in their star system, and are about three times Earth's radius with about 8 times Earth's mass. This is a field with active exploration and a lot we can expect to learn over the next few decades.
